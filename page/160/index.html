Elegy for a Country’s Seasons by Zadie Smith

There is the scientific and ideological language for what is happening to the weather, but there are hardly any intimate words. Is that surprising? People in mourning tend to use euphemism; likewise the guilty and ashamed. The most melancholy of all the euphemisms: “The new normal.” “It’s the new normal,” I think, as a beloved pear tree, half-drowned, loses its grip on the earth and falls over. The train line to Cornwall washes away—the new normal. We can’t even say the word “abnormal” to each other out loud: it reminds us of what came before. Better to forget what once was normal, the way season followed season, with a temperate charm only the poets appreciated.
The weather has changed, is changing, and with it so many seemingly small things—quite apart from train tracks and houses, livelihoods and actual lives—are being lost. It was easy to assume, for example, that we would always be able to easily find a hedgehog in some corner of a London garden, pick it up in cupped hands, and unfurl it for our children—or go on a picnic and watch fat bumblebees crawling over the mouth of an open jam jar. Every country has its own version of this local sadness. (And every country has its version of our arguments, when it comes to causation.

“I think I should clarify by mentioning that my class is not so a much a practical class as it is a class based on discussions of cell phone photography as a social phenomenon. While respecting the time-honored tradition of fine art photography, I think we can recognize that cell phone photography has impacted the way we communicate visually, notions of artistic authority, and crowd-driven practices such as citizen journalism. The class does NOT suggest cell phone photography as a replacement for professional or fine art photography. For students seeking that kind of instruction, I usually recommend a traditional photography class. What my class does is examine a widespread practice (both its benefits and its detriments) that has changed the way we use and interact with images.”

—

Jenny Odell, replying to some comments on Wired’s story about her current exhibition in San Francisco which took their cue from the captions to one of the images, which mentioned that she’s teaching a course in cell phone photography.

Obviously, you should never read the comments, and the rest of them are particularly bad (“Help me out here: Point, Click. Is there anything more than that?”; “On the other hand, Stanford gives credit for a lot of courses: FEMGEN 140A: Destroying Dichotomies: Exploring Multiple Sex, Gender, and Sexual Identities”), but I’m still glad I read them to find Odell sticking up for the course, which sounds genuinely interesting and useful.

“Briefly stated, the Gell-Mann Amnesia effect is as follows. You open the newspaper to an article on some subject you know well. In Murray’s case, physics. In mine, show business. You read the article and see the journalist has absolutely no understanding of either the facts or the issues. Often, the article is so wrong it actually presents the story backward—reversing cause and effect. I call these the “wet streets cause rain” stories. Paper’s full of them.
In any case, you read with exasperation or amusement the multiple errors in a story, and then turn the page to national or international affairs, and read as if the rest of the newspaper was somehow more accurate about Palestine than the baloney you just read. You turn the page, and forget what you know.”

— Michael Crichton (via betaknowledge)
