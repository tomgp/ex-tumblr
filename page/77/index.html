Monday, December 22, 2014

“Dynamic and wildly profitable Internet companies like Facebook and Google may get most of the attention, but Silicon Valley is littered with firms that just get by doing roughly the same thing year after year — has-beens like Ask.com, a search engine that no longer innovates but happily takes in $400 million in annual revenue, turning a profit in the process.”

—

Nicholas Carlson: What Happened When Marissa Mayer Tried to Be Steve Jobs (via iamdanw)

Apparently being a quietly, reliably, profitable company is a bad thing nowadays. Capitalism! Don’t you love it?

(via blech) Ill-informed hunch: Haven’t read the article but I’m guessing the thing here is that “wildly profitable internet companies like Facebook and Google” don’t pay their shareholders dividends where normally profitable companies like Ask.com do, so there’s a reason why you’d hold onto Ask.com shares even if the company’s not getting any bigger whereas in the absence of dividends Google and Facebook shareholders are looking to sell their shares at a much higher price than they bought them in order to realise a return on their investment. So Yahoo!’s problem – I’m guessing – boils down to it being difficult to move from being that kind of high growth company to being one which pays its investors more modest dividends without seeing their share price tank and thus their ability to pay those dividends compromised. As I said though, not read the article so I may be getting the wrong end of the stick.

(Source: iamdanw, via blech)
