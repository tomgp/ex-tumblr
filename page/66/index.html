The Lifecycle of Software Objects by Ted Chiang

I’ve been meaning to read this for a while – recommended by Dion years and years ago – I also got round to reading a bunch his short stories but more on them another time.

A novella* about an out of work zoo keeper who goes to work for a company training ‘digients’ – simple AI’s that live inside a virtual world which is a bit like Second Life (remember that? that’s something that dates this story) and her relationship with one such in particular. Not really my kind of sci-fi, it pursues a kind of technological realism that I just don’t find particularly interesting, the kind of sci-fi writer as as futurist agenda that’s what people often latch onto about the genre: “Arthur C Clarke predicted satellites so these books have value!” type thing. That said Ted Chiang’s an impressive writer, his day job producing technical documentation and his legendarily languid publication schedule (a batch of short stories numbering in the low teens in a 25 year career) gives the prose a rare kind of fine tuned neutrality that suits the subject matter well; you can be sure that every word is necessary and correct. And the real motor of the story is some pleasingly low key human drama rather than the technology which stops the whole thing being an exercise in dry extrapolation – i mean there is plenty of dry extrapolation but its fairly well hidden; the whole Second Life setting is kind of an object lesson in the pitfalls of this apporach.

* I don’t like the term novella but this is pretty much the text book definition.
