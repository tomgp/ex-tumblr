On The Midnight Shift

tomewing:

judgeanon:

It’s not a mega epic. It doesn’t introduce any new characters, plot elements or huge sweeping changes to the setting. Its entire premise is literally “Just another night at the office” for Dredd and the judges of Mega-City One. And yet, The Graveyard Shift, by John Wagner, Alan Grant and Ron Smith, may just be one of the most important stories of the 1980s classic Judge Dredd era.

Keep reading

Great piece on one of the quietly key Dredd stories (and probably the best showcase for classic Dredd artist Ron Smith, too).

I never liked Ron Smith when I was reading 2000AD regularly as a child but I think he might be my favourite Dredd artist these days (though can’t say I have much exposure to Dredd post prog 900 or so). Not for the way he draws the main character which I still don’t really like that much, but for the way he draws the city and the citzens. With regard to the former; he manages to cram a huge amount of detail in without the layouts gettign crowded, often jamming different perspectives hard up against each other in a way that makes the whole thing look perfectly natural and yet slightly headache inducing. He pulls a similar trick with his Mega Citizens; they often have a slightly retro 60s/70s look about them (one of the things I used to dislike), a kind of agressive normality, but then Smith freezes them in unflatering positions, their faces grotesque (the lady on the right of that cover is a good example). The closest comparison I can think of is Dan Clowes, though I get the feeling Clowes has more sympathy for his creations. The collection of Dredd Daily Star strips he produced is certainly one of my most heavily thumbed collections.
