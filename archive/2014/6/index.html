“I’m old enough that I’ve seen the same debates go round and round a few times.

One is the (always well-intentioned) cry - let’s stop saying customers/consumers/users, let’s remember they’re people! This always snags an emotional latch but I think it’s worth resisting.

Firstly, let’s remember that they’re also mammals - does that help? No. Moving up to the next biggest category isn’t especially useful.

Secondly, if you need reminding that your customers/consumers/users are people you have bigger problems. Changing what you write on your briefs/stories isn’t going to help.

Thirdly, some linguistic precision about your relationship with your people is, I think, useful. Part of what went wrong in public services was the wholesale import of a ‘customer mindset’ - thinking it meant the creation of a service culture. It didn’t.

User is a good word because it clearly indicates what the relationship is all about. Our primary responsibility is to make something that someone can use. It’s about utility.

”—

Russell Davies Consumers, users, people, mammals.

Yeah, I’m old enough too.

“Excellence in statistical graphics consists of complex ideas communicated with clarity, precision, and efficiency. Graphical displays should

* show the data

* induce the viewer to think about the substance rather than about methodology, graphic design, the technology of graphic production or something else

* avoid distorting what the data have to say

* present many numbers in a small space

* make large data sets coherent

* encourage the eye to compare different pieces of data

* reveal the data at several levels of detail, from broad overview to the fine structure

* serve a reasonably clear purpose: description, exploration, tabulation, or decoration

* be closely integrated with statistical and verbal descriptions of the data set.

#film cakes #david lynch

legochesters:

Arguing about books vs. ebooks is like arguing about cake vs. also cake.

I disagree.

I mean it’s not an argument as such but printed books and ebooks have totally different affordances. So…

ebooks With an ebook you can look up any words you don’t know easily (on my kindle at least there’s a couple of dictionaries), you can search annotations to the text more easily and save chunks of the text more easily, and you can more easily get to a precise place in the book based on certain words phrases etc, not to mention the fact that you can carry about a vast library of books in your pocket.

printed books With a printed book you can give it to someone when you’re finished, you can flick backwards and forwards much more easily apprehending the shape of a text or the structure of its argument. You can make more complex annotations more quickly. There’s also evidence that people read more slowly and have lower comprehension and retention when reading on a screen. (c.f. The Reading Brain in the Digital Age: The Science of Paper versus Screens)

As you can probably tell I prefer printed books – though at any time I tend to be reading stuff in both formats (much easier to read on a phone when you’re eating lunch), I think I’ll always own a epaper kindle or equiv.

But yeah. If you can’t tell the difference between the ebook type of cake and the physical book type of cake I wouldn’t trust you to make me a book cake… er… or something.
