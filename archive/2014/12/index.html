“Of course all cities have their hidden themes and influences — New York has its Mob, Rome its Christian Democrats, London its Old Boy Network, Singapore its Triads, Dublin its Republican Army, all working away there, out of sight and generally out of thought, to determine the character of the place. The unseen forms of Hav, though, seem to me harder to define than any, so vague are they, so insidious, and I find it difficult to enunciate the feeling this is beginning to leave in my mind. It is a tantalizing and disquieting sensation. It is rather like the taste you get in the butter, if it has been close to other foods in the refrigerator; or like the dark calculating look that cats sometimes give you; or the sudden silence that falls when you walk into a room where they are talking about you; or like one of those threadbare exhausting dreams that have you groping through an impenetrable tangle of time, space and meaning, looking for your car keys.”—

Hav, Jan Morris. (via ryansara)

I’ve got a really nice hardback copy of this but I’m sorely tempted to get the new edition; both for the Ursula Le Guin foreword and the fact that NYRB editions are excellent.

“Dynamic and wildly profitable Internet companies like Facebook and Google may get most of the attention, but Silicon Valley is littered with firms that just get by doing roughly the same thing year after year — has-beens like Ask.com, a search engine that no longer innovates but happily takes in $400 million in annual revenue, turning a profit in the process.”—

Nicholas Carlson: What Happened When Marissa Mayer Tried to Be Steve Jobs (via iamdanw)

Apparently being a quietly, reliably, profitable company is a bad thing nowadays. Capitalism! Don’t you love it?

(via blech) Ill-informed hunch: Haven’t read the article but I’m guessing the thing here is that “wildly profitable internet companies like Facebook and Google” don’t pay their shareholders dividends where normally profitable companies like Ask.com do, so there’s a reason why you’d hold onto Ask.com shares even if the company’s not getting any bigger whereas in the absence of dividends Google and Facebook shareholders are looking to sell their shares at a much higher price than they bought them in order to realise a return on their investment. So Yahoo!’s problem – I’m guessing – boils down to it being difficult to move from being that kind of high growth company to being one which pays its investors more modest dividends without seeing their share price tank and thus their ability to pay those dividends compromised. As I said though, not read the article so I may be getting the wrong end of the stick.

Filtered for nematodes and Uniqlo (17 Dec., 2014, at Interconnected)interconnected.org

One of the best internet things this year is that Matt Webb, founder of the late Berg, has started updating his blog again – crammed with interesting stuff that feels like its all related in some way just beyond my ability to apprehend. The stuff here is particularly interesting to me because of the nematode worms thing I mean I’m fascinated be nematode worms in general, but in particular, this stuff about recreating their connectome in the light of last nights Black Mirror episode which had people being uploaded to household ‘smart’ devices etc.

I really enjoyed that program, I mean Charlie Brooker does really well at taking these reasonably common but perhaps televisually difficult sci-fi ideas and sticking them in a mainstream TV show so they’re totally comprehensible. In last nights Xmas special; the ethical/moral dimension of the cloned brain uploading stuff (I’m not going in to it here you should really try and watch the program) was really well done 1. reminded me of The Ones Who Walk Away From Omelas 2. Often in TV/ cinematic sci-fi there’s a feeling that society (in the fiction) hasn’t discussed the ideas that the fiction is presenting to the viewer – the characters in BSG don’t seem to have a history of thinking about what constitutes consciousness, the differences between cylons and humans etc. In Black Mirror the societies do seem to have had these conversations and generally to have arrived at morally unpleasant/ difficult positions for populist reasons or simply as a matter of convenience.

“

Every morning, the Sim “agents,” as they are called, wake up, arrive at whatever workplace needs laborers (within their programmed class bracket), and toil in this industrial zone until dinner. Every evening, they go to sleep at whatever random house slots them a space. Life as a Sim is lived in this constant present tense, shuttling through traffic to take whatever work or shelter they can get.

“Sims are selfless little troopers, working at any place they are required,” explains the designer of their algorithm on the SimCity website. “Even if their home was destroyed, they will take up occupancy in a new home all in the name of keeping the city running as effectively as possible.”

While this designer probably intended for these Sims to add texture to the game’s design, what he programmed is eerie: an endless loop of faceless, nameless labor. What’s terrifying about these “selfless little troopers” is not their strangeness but their familiarity. This nightmarish vision of a world where we’re all temps, deskilled and interchangeable, called up daily by different bosses, is already a reality for many.

The police truncheon is precisely the point where the state’s bureaucratic imperative for imposing simple administrative schema, and its monopoly of coercive force, come together.
