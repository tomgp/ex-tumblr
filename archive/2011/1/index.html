“In other words: we can’t exploit this legitimate source of revenue, because then people will stop giving us more money. You sometimes see similar arguments against deaccessioning in the US: we can’t let museums sell work because then rich people will cut back on their giving. You can’t make this stuff up.”—

Donn Zaretsky on arguments against deaccessioning.

I get such a kick out of him.

(via hydeordie)

My take: The formulation of the argument that Zaretsky is attacking ignores the fact that by selling artifacts the value of the museum as a whole decreases. Follwing the route of encouraging selling off their collections to pay rent will, in the end, leave us with no museums.

To be clear, I think museums are a good thing because of the social value that they provide by making valuable cultural artefacts available to the widest possible audience not becasue of their profitablility or money making potential, those concerns should only be important insofar as they facilitate that provision.

… also the idea of not exploiting one source of revenue because it threatens another is hardly restricted to museums.

“Does this mean usable software is bad? No. Software where usability is key, for example, ATMs, information websites or home video editing apps will not benefit from Internalization since the tasks involved will likely be casual and incidental. Remember, the tasks still get done just fine with the more helpful interfaces, they simply push more of the work on the interface and involve less learning, which isn’t required in the above circumstances. The findings of the study are more applicable to serious task environments where learning itself is the aim. Nimwegen suggests to take care with externalizing especially in tasks with: frequent interruptions, educational objectives, skill transfer, high costs, continuous attention and deep domain understanding.”—

The Dark Side Of Usability

I’ve been wondering about similar things re. information graphics, should we introduce a certain amount of friction in the design to induce the viewer to engage more with the content? Seems to me slick design might cause people to focus on surface aspects, accepting a series of facts, rather than fully engaging with the subject matter. Some of the best info-graphics ( off the top of my head I’m thinking Minard, Snow and french railway timetables here ) require a degree of reader engagement higher than your typical modern infographic, the pay off is a better understanding of the subject matter. I guess it’s about drawing the viewer in without smoothing out all the features of the data/system you’re trying to illustrate. Further reading Easy = True

Bloodless Coop: Shop Class as Soulcraft: lessons for agency and endeavourfarmerversusfox.tumblr.com

Worth reading: Alex F’s notes on “Shopclass as Soulcraft”

farmerversusfox:

manual work presents us with problems that are material and unavoidable…

When it is done, we can see the outcome as correct - though we may need to check our working before signing off on it. This suggests not dull work but something akin to mathematics, whose problems are readily regarded as some of the highest intellectual pursuits within our culture.

As someone who makes software this raises questions for me about how it fits the definition of ‘Knowledge work’ being talked about here in opposition to manual work…

It seems obvious that computer programming is knowledge work but it fits neatly with this understanding of manual work as well, so long as you consider data, maths and boolean logic to be your raw materials. Its roots in maths maybe showing through?

meanwhile

You commission market research, which suggests calling your phone a Meetbox may go down well with a desired demographic.

… [but after all is said and done] …

We don’t have a firm feedback, we can’t say this was a success or failure - except of course, in the eyes of others. 

Doesn’t sound dissimilar from someone hand tooling a chair, I mean you do have a chair at the end and you can say you’re satisfied more or less with how comfortable it is but you don’t really know if a different plan or a better craftsman would have made something more ergonomic or more durable. Is the scale (i.e. large operation vs small) making the difference rather than whether the work is traditionally manual or not?

This struck me particularly…

Crawford argues that as the tangible aspects of work wilt away, we are left focusing on managing our personal brand and becoming more involved in maintaining the best face, from all angles, in the hall of mirrors that is the workplace. In Dancing in the Streets, Barbara Ehrenreich recounts how the noble class within Europe  moved from a culture of individual endeavour - warrior-lords leading their men directly through prowess - to one of social surfaces, of courtiers concerned with intrigue and alliance. The engine for this was the centralisation of military power, requiring nobles to define themselves around something other than martial skill. This process continues now with the knowledge class.

The exhortations of marketing experts that we should develop a personal brand never really sat comfortably with me and this is some interesting context (c.f. Erenreich’s Bait and Swith for more stuff in this area)

Looking forward to the next instalment.

(also the idea of ’doing as thinking’ is interesting)
