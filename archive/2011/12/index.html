“

Only… supposing that this malevolent AI comes from a military source is pure Hollywood. Such systems are built with high priority to systematic reporting, accountability, multiple redundancies, fail-safes and obedience to chain of command. No, there are other complex computer systems that seem far more likely to suddenly become self-aware in powerfully dangerous ways.

Take those high-speed trading systems we’ve been discussing. They are growing incredibly sophisticated, at a very rapid rate, absorbing and incorporating models of human psychology, with one goal in mind. To appraise and predict behavior patterns in order for the program to track and to pounce on opportunities for predatory trading. Competitive ferocity is the only criterion for success. Indeed, if you were to even propose inserting balancing factors like ethics or morality or accountability into such a project, you’d at-minimum be laughed down and probably fired.

Moreover, these systems are receiving billions in funding (including their own new transatlantic fiber cable) entirely in secret. There are no public agencies involved. No third party observers. No Congressional oversight committees. No supervision whatsoever. Laboratories developing new genetic strains of wheat are under closer accountability than cryptic Wall Street think tanks that may unleash the first fully autonomous AI… programmed deliberately to have only the behavior patterns, goals, attitudes and morality of parasites.

”—

David Brin on why a Robin Hood Tax is our best weapon against the Terminator. (The rest of his post is less far-fetched but this is still the best bit.)

This reminds me of the best ideas in Charlie Stross’s otherwise pretty so-so Accelerando: All matter in the ‘post singularity’ solar system is being reconfigured into computers upon which sophisticated AI’s run riot. The AIs in question are all descendants of financial and legal instruments.

“A cheeseburger cannot exist outside of a highly developed, post-agrarian society. It requires a complex interaction between a handful of vendors—in all likelihood, a couple of dozen—and the ability to ship ingredients vast distances while keeping them fresh. The cheeseburger couldn’t have existed until nearly a century ago as, indeed, it did not”—

“On the impracticality of a cheeseburger” by Waldo Jaquith. (Via Daring Fireball.)

mmmm… Cheesburger.

But hold on, the leap from the impossibility of trying to make all the ingredients for a cheeseburger from scratch to saying that it can only exist in a post agrarian socity is a pretty big one. People have always had neighbours and specialisms, working together to produce something isn’t a luxury of ‘highly developed’ post-agrarian society. One person actually being able to single handedly produce a cheeseburger from scratch is - however impractical - surely a much rarer thing in human history.

Tangent - this reminded me of a great bit Red Plenty (one of my favourite books of the last few years - thanks Kate!) where Kruschev visits New York and admires the hamburger

Of all the capitalist countries, it was America that was most nearly trying to do the same time as the Soviet Union. They shared the Soviet insight… Take the hamburger: so neat, so easy. It had been created by someone who had made it their serious mission in life to imagine a food you could hold in your fist while you rushed thought the busy city. And this was not exceptional for America, it was characteristic. If you looked into the windows of their shops, if you looked at the advertisements in their magazines, you saw the same practical passion at work everywhere. Coca-Cola bottles exactly fitted the average person’s hand. Bandages came as a packet of pink patches with a glue just strong enough for the human skin already applied to each one. America was a torrent of clever anticipations. Soviet industries would have to learn to anticipate as cleverly, more cleverly, if they were to overtake America in satisfying wants as well as needs. They too would have to become experts in everyday desire.

Notes on "Personality, Intelligence and Expertise: Impacts on software dev. Jo E Hannay"

Hi, I’m reading Making Software at the moment. A pretty dry review of the literature surrounding empirical research in software development i.e. what makes for good quality software in terms of techniques, methodologies etc. I intend to blog the bits that are interesting for my own reference. This process is likely to take months as my stomach for academic writing is weak. Those of you not into software dev may wish to look away now.

Personality:

Personality does not seem to be a very strong predictor of performance. Lets’ briefly look at what effects there are. Conscientiousness is, in general, the most persistent of the personality factors when it comes to predicting both academic and job performance [schmidt and hunter 1988]. Guess what: this seems not to be the case for pair programming [Saleh et al. 2010]. In fact, in our data, we even found that conscientiousness had a negative effect (also for solo programmers). What seems to have a positive effect is openness to experience [Saleh et al. 2010] and difference in extraversion [Hannay et al. 2010]. Pairs whose peers have different levels of extraversion work quicker than those with more similar levels… peers with different levels of extraversion collaborate more intensely

Later on same: Disruption bad for a team but total lack of debate ( where debate = mild disruption) is worse “pairs or teams with personalities that are too alike will lead to lack of debate … [Williams et al. 2006][Walle and HAnnay 2009]”

Effort estimation:

Not even close to knowing how to do improve this, the author suggests environmental factors help. Examples

  • Delete irrelevant information from requirements docs
  • Avoid tentative base estimates which distort thinking, ask people for ideal estimates of effort before asking them for likely estimates of effort [Jorgenson 2005]
  • Group estimation is better than individual estimation (‘planning poker’ etc I guess)
  • Estimates associated with iterative dev. more accurate than those associates with relay-style [Molokken-Ostvold and Jorgenson 2005]

Adaptive behaviour - acknowledge shortcomings and choose strategy accordingly is relevant here. “Approaches to focus on the few most critical factors for software effort estimation include analogical reasoning and top-down estimation* [Li et al. 2007],[Jorgensen 2004], [HErtwig et al. 1999]

LAter suggests improving programming skill may by proxy improve estimation skill

  • Also - Pair programming, improves code production by improving social environment rather than coders themselves. Most beneficial for novices on complex tasks. (there’s a whole chapter in this later). 

Collaboration:

Lots of theories available in this domain, too often bolted onto results post hoc - there is always some theory that fits. Pair programming again:

tentative results are that spending more time discussing the task description leads to less time spent overall in solving the task… [no evidence but rather this should be taken as] … input to your reflective process as a practitioner.

*  Precise definition useful I think [via]… Once you have established an overall estimate for the project, you sub-divide it down through the layers of the work breakdown structure, for example, development will be 50% of the total, testing will be 25% etc; then sub-divide development and testing into their components etc etc etc.
