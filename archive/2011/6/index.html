How do you decide what to get out of the library?

tomewing:

““But where the larger query is concerned, I really don’t have a clue. How can I build an evaluative hierarchy that works in the absence of the capitalist framework through which I evaluated music throughout the majority of my life?””

—

http://andrewtsks.tumblr.com/post/6968086268/a-friend-of-mine-confided-he-can-now-no-longer-be

How do you decide what to get out of the library? This sounds snarky but it’s a serious question around this whole water-water-everywhere line of argument. Assuming you have access to a library (which is an assumption I probably shouldn’t make, since I have no idea about how the US library system works), you are confronted at quite a young age with the problem of there being vastly more information available to you, for free, than you have hours to absorb it. The library problem isn’t exactly the same as the free music problem but they share enough points that it’s an interesting comparison.

True, I think the problem is maybe that people are acting as if they were a library acquisitions department rather than a member of the public choosing what to get out of the library. Spotify really helped me get my head around this, i.e. all of a sudden I didn’t need to download stuff when I saw it, you know, just incase a torrent went down or other P2P network was taken off line or a blgger took down the files, or whatever. It gave me back the freedom to going back to only considering stuff I wanted to listen to rather than stuff that in the future I might potentially want to listen to.

Favoured media products 2010

Just found this in the draft section, don’t know why I didn’t post it. Possibly becasue I wanted to write about games and music and films too.

Books:

The Cave - Jose Saramgo

Ursula Le guin’s theory about Saramago is that his books which prominently feature a dog are his best.

Saramago’s reminder to us that we aren’t the be-all and end-all of creation is, usually, a dog. I developed a simple ranking system for his fiction: the books with a dog are better than the ones with no dog; the more important the dog, the better the book.

I agree. The Cave, is wonderful book, a tragic story that’s ultimately hopeful.

Magic For Beginners/ Pretty Monsters - Kelly Link

Disappointingly there’s some duplication in these two short story collections but really I didn’t mind reading the best bits of Magic For Beginners again. These two volumes contain some of the creepiest and funniest and weirdest and truest writing I’ve read. Interestingly I found Pretty Monsters in the children’s/ young adult section in WH Smiths. Kid’s are reading this stuff? That gives me hope for the future.

Complete Cosmicomics - Italo Calvino

Transmutes science into myths. If you like Italo Calvino you’ll love this! Also, does anyone else see the similarity between Calvino’s protagonist Qfwfq and Q out of Star Trek TNG?

Red Plenty - Francis Spufford

A non-fiction novel with the soviet economy under Kruschev as it’s theme. Much better than that sounds! (unless it sounds really good to you, in which case: Excatly as good as it sounds!) There’s a couple of particularly brilliant chapters where Spufford follows the operation of simple components first inside an early computer then later in cell replication.

All Star Superman - Grant Morrison, Frank Quitely

I like superhero comics best when they’re bright and fun and out-there. I mean I like Watchmen obviously but many of the lesser series that followed it, taking it’s “darkness” and “realism” and missing the point entirely don’t do anything for me. Grant Morrison gets why this is.

As Michael Chabon said in his keynote at the ‘04 Eisner awards

Twist endings, the unexpected usefulness of unlikely knowledge, nobility and bravery where it’s least expected, and the sudden emergence of a thread of goodness in a wicked nature, those were the kind of stories told by the writers and artists of the comic books that I liked.

…

an intricate, involved, involving mythology that is also accessible, comprehensible, at any point of entry

…

The key, as in baroque music, is repetition with variation.

…

A mind is not blown, in spite of whatever Hollywood seems to teach, merely by action sequences, things exploding, thrilling planetscapes, wild bursts of speed. Those are good things. But a mind is blown when something you always feared but knew to be impossible turns out to be true; when the world turns out to be far vaster, far more marvelous or malevolent than you ever dreamed; when you get proof that everything is connected to everything else, that everything you know is wrong, that you are both the center of the universe and a tiny speck sailing off its nethermost edge.

All Star Superman has all that stuff and more.

tentacular:

In 2011, the British Con-Dem coalition government imposed massive cuts to public spending, ostensibly to reduce the national deficit. The funding shortfalls produced by this austerity programme were to be met by opening up essential public services –schools, hospitals, universities, hospitals, libraries, and so on – to corporate investment and, where the profitability was likely too be too small or too distant in time, voluntary work within the affected communities.  This latter option, known as the Big Society initiative, met with little success and was quietly dropped from political and news agendas. Not, however, before introducing the country to an array of costumed crimefighters and, eventually, a handful of genuine heroes.

Memos and recordings of secret high level meetings leaked to the press in 2015 show that, in an attempt to reduce the cuts to the police service, senior officers conspired to provoke the wave of protests sweeping the UK into violence. They reasoned that the greater the threat to property – one tape reveals officers agreeing to use ‘public order’ as a euphemism – the more likely corporate bosses were to pressurise politicians into maintaining, perhaps even expanding, the police budget. This strategy proved disastrous. Many aspects of police work were suddenly opened up to competitive tender, with tax-payers’ money diverted into the coffers of multinational security consultant companies. The size of the police force was massively reduced. Many former officers found themselves employed by these new ‘security providers’ as freelancers or on short-term contracts, doing the same work for little more than minimum wage.  Only the least profitable of police work – crimes against people, particularly in the poorest  sectors of society – were left to the police force.

Meanwhile, the Big Society initiative encouraged neighbourhood watch schemes and other community groups to police their own streets. And while many people were concerned about the violence and injustices this introduced, the media lapped it up. Steven Seagal presented four seasons of the reality TV series Have-A-Go Heroes, a ratings hit that inspired numerous imitators,  including Ross Kemp’s Britain’s Hardest Heroes and Danny Dyer’s Village Vigilantes. Richard Branson, Simon Cowell, Andrew Lloyd-Webber and Alan Sugar joined forces to produce Britain’s Got Talents, a show which uncovered the nation’s would-be superheroes, and The X-Factory, which followed each season’s finalists as through superhero boot camp. For a while, every school-child wanted to be the next Wicca Man, EastEnder, White Van Man, Hammer or CiderMan, the west country cyborg.
