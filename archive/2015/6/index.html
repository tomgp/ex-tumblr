#total perspective vortex

Snowpiercer, unstructured thoughts.

Definitely Miévillian – I mean violent class struggle on a train.

Took me about 45 mins to realise the main guy was captain America.

Afterwards I went online and read what people thought and there was a whole lot about how the film’s last act was rendered incomprehensible because the Korean dialogue wasn’t subtitled which kind of opened my eyes to how cine-illiterate yr average Internet forum guy must be. No wonder people go crazy for those MCU films. SPOILERS: I mean even without the benefit of flashbacks it’s pretty damn obvious why you might want to blow a hole in the side of a train that you’ve been stuck on for 17years even if it means probable death, especially so if you happen to be chemically dependant and have spent a large chunk of those 17 yrs in a dark box like one of those ones they have in morgues (in American hospitals stations on the tele)

Sci-if sub genre wise it’s kind of a generation ship story. Particularly reminded of the central conflict in the Le Guin novella that closes her ‘birthday of the world’ collection I.e. Those who have developed a religion based on the perfection of the sealed ecology of the ship vs those who are like ‘sod it, just drop us off on the nearest thing with an atmosphere’

The fact of the train raised some interesting possibilities re the extent to which any individual might know their position in the train and by extension in the grand class scheme of things. Like how far the train extends back and forwards from a given passenger and the extent over which they’re able to range and the implications of this – does this reflect how class works, to what extent do we only encounter people in adjacent carriages? Etc.

generally speaking The train/ class thing works really well; obviously a massive simplification but it has a way of not quite adding up in interesting ways.

But yeah, glad I finally got round to watching it.

Beware the Camden kink

leftoverlondoner:

London Transport has unveiled its night tube map which is in effect the usual map with non-night lines removed. This has resulted in a horrible graphic quirk - the Camden kink:

As there is no night service on the Northern Line city branch, there’s nothing to balance the lurch to the west below Camden Town. Easily straightened, but at the moment, very ugly.

agreed, removing lines from the map is a great opportunity to fix some of the problems that have developed over the years (the central, and jubilee line’s rambling paths in particular), simply hiding the unused parts of the system is pretty lazy (though there’s an argument that keeping places in the familiar locations is helpful – I don’t buy that personally)

For comparison, check out Maxwell Roberts’ hexalinear version the glowing yellow text on black background is obviously dreaful but the layout is really clear.

What is code? If you don't know, you need to read this:bloomberg.com

So I read this whole magazine article (it’s the whole of businessweek this week) that’s like 35000(~+/-) words long – i was suspiscious of whether this would be a good use of my time. It’s pretty good. Paul Ford is a good writter. It’s basically got three interleaved bits:

  1. an explanation of what is involved, mechanically speaking, in writing computer programs, that kind of segues into an overview of popular/ historically important programing languages – good stuff insofar as I agree with the points made and can see it would be genuinely helpful in understanding all that crap.

  2. kind of moving up the chain from programing languages themselves to the communities that exist around them, conferences etc. and the sort of meta communities like Stack Overflow and so on, how those thing interact and how they inform development & design methodologies &c. This is probably the bit I thought was most interesting, I think the cultrural environment in which programming/coding takes place is not really examined enough, this is a good start.

  3. Then interleaved with this is a sort of office story about making some software which leavens the whole thing, illustrates some of the points made in 1 and 2 and generally provides the macro structure for the piece. There’s a bit of a management book vibe to this part, if you have to ever had to read a management book for a proffesional studies course at uni, or maybe you were really bored at an exeptoinally poorly stocked airport bookshop, some of the tropes here will be familiar (heroic manager fails to understand some thing about people he has to manage then learns some lessons and does some sucessfull managing: Bonus! Promotion! Respect of peers!) – i guess that makes sense for the typical Business Week reader. Personally I’d have liked that bit, bit 3, to be a more critical nd less generic buiness parable. I mean most software projects fail and this isn’t really disucussed, that’s a big cultural feature of software development.

So, yes, probably worth reading though I don’t think you’re going to be left behind if you don’t (as the strapline up there suggests) so don’t worry too much (also, read it on your phone or you’ll be constantly distracted by glitchy gimicks (some of which are actually pretty cool) (though on iOS safari the footnotes don’t work))
