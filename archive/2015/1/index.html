A request to Manics fans out thererhianejones.com

This year I’ll be writing a book on Manics album The Holy Bible, along with Larissa Wodtke and Daniel Lukes. My section will focus on the album’s social and political context ie Britain in the 90s, the album’s appeal to teenagers, and reasons why the band had such a huge female fanbase. As part of researching this book, I’d find it helpful to speak to other fans of the band about the album – both those who, like myself, grew up with the Manics in the 90s, and those who discovered them later. (If you’re interested in what I’ve written previously on the band, most of it is here.) If you’d be willing to tell me a bit about your experience as a Manics fan, please reply to this post with a contact email or, if you prefer, contact me yourself on theholybibletriptych@gmail.com. Thank you!

innowheres:

Wolf Hall was pretty decent, eh?

Yeah, the staging and casting was great – though Damien Lewis looks too small in spite of the best efforts of the camera angles. It felt to me pretty faithful but also really different to the source (obviously, inevitably because its a 6 part TV series rather than the first two novels in a trilogy); really sparse when the books are so dense and byzantine. I guess we were always going to miss Cromwell’s mental machinations and weighing up of events and so on. But yeah, I’ll be watching the rest.

cf I Claudius book vs TV prog.

Aside: The BBC seem desperate to make it a big event with historians talking about it on News Night (which I didn’t watch) and a hashtag (which I didn’t follow) and so on but the actual thing felt pretty low key (in a good way).

I Can Never Have Too Many Mechanical Pencilstheatlantic.com

The better your mechanical pencil, the more forlorn you feel when you lose it, as you will inevitably do, for pencils of any kind fall into that class of objects—along with umbrellas, cigarette lighters, and, perhaps, sunglasses—that are somehow more often lost than found, and so never quite permanently the property of any one individual. To spend tens of dollars, then, on a single pencil, let alone hundreds or even thousands (which is eminently possible, should you require a barrel of solid silver or a more precious metal), might seem a quixotic form of tool fetishism. Yet by the same token it is also an act of aesthetic defiance, in the face of the pencil-swallowing Absurd.

I’m very much a net loser of such objects but E_ is a rare (possibly unique?) net-gainer. Her job has her visiting lots of different offices around the place and she always seems to manage to accumulate stationery, we have several overflowing shoeboxes – yes, I married “the pencil-swallowing Absurd”.
