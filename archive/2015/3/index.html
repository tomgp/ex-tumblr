It occurs to me that failure to properly worldbuild an SFFnal story is - sometimes, though not always - less reflective of a writer’s creative ability than it is a consequence of their real-world privilege. The concept of culture as something with multiple…

Worth reading the linked post I think – cropped here so people don’t have the whole long thing on their dashboard again – we follow some of the same people maybe, so when long text posts do the rounds…

Anyway: I’m totally sympathetic to the gist of the original post and am a terrible bore about such things on a regular basis.

So, yes, but also no. Lazy world building is bad, sure, a sign of writerly privilege? Possibly; a lack of empathy and imagination can definitely come from a being in a position of power “structures of inequality produce lopsided structures of the imagination” and all that. But I’m not convinced that’s what the potatos of Middle Earth are. I think they’re mainly  a narrative shorthand, Sam mentions them because what they represent to the (presumed) reader is what Tolkien intends them to represent to Sam, a certain homeliness, mundanity etc. but also Sam wants to make fish and chips which have a particular place in the British imagination*. I think that’s how a lot of world building needs to work… you put the effort in to the bits you want to draw attention to (mythology, legend + language in the case of LOTR ) and the rest should be inconspicuous sometimes it’s the writer’s job to get out of the way of the reader. 

In short I agree with the arguments put forward but think Tolkien is not a great example.

* It has even been suggested that fish and chips helped win World War I.
According to Professor John Walton, author of Fish and Chips and the British Working Class, the government made safeguarding supplies a priority.


During World War II, ministers bent over backwards to make sure fish and chips were one of the few foods that were never rationed.
…
George Orwell in The Road to Wigan Pier (1937) put fish and chips first among the home comforts that helped keep the masses happy and “averted revolution”.
