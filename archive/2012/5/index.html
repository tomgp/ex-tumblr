Fighting people who are wrong on the internet!

I try not to argue on the internet too much for all the obvious reasons but combating innumeracy with graphics is like my dream Jeopardy category so here goes:

James on twitter drew my attention to this quote from a paper by the Center for Social Justice (note I haven’t read most of the paper):

The first methodological flaw of the Government’s central measure of poverty is that it is defined in relative terms. The result of this is that the poor will always exist statistically, as it is inevitable that some in society will have less than others.

It isn’t the first time that someone who gets paid to understand this stuff has failed to understand this stuff. Here’s Frank “poverty czar” Field back in 2010:

Any candidate sitting GCSE maths should be able to explain that raising everybody above a set percentage of median income is rather like asking a cat to catch its own tail. As families are raised above the target level of income, the median point itself rises.

Actually any candidate sitting GCSE maths should be able to tell you that both these quotes are wrong.

Here is a chart illustrating how using a relative measure of poverty (the one we actually use, below 60% median income = poor) you might distribute incomes so that no one would be in poverty (note to the CSJ: Don’t worry, some people still have more  money than others!):

External image

There are lots of other ways you can achieve this but this is the one which requires pretty much the least adjustment from the current distribution of incomes. You can play around with the chart and find your own preferred distribution here.

The median is actually a bit of a tricky concept to get an intuitive grasp of, one of the reasons that this simple interactive chart is one of my favourite things I’ve built is that 1. it didn’t take very long and 2. it really shows how well interactive graphics can make abstract concepts more tangible, allowing people to explore a system’s possibility space*.

That said, however difficult the idea of the median might be there’s not really any excuse for people who are paid to understand this stuff not understanding it (esp if they’re being paid by the tax payer and/ or their lack of understanding might condemn people to lives of actual physical hardship and early death). I mean, to write a critique of the way in which something is measured without actually having a reasonable grasp of that measurement kind of undermines any conclusions you might come to don’t you think?

Second, this mega-rich elite, containing many of the bankers and hedge fund and private equity operators who caused the financial crash in the first place, have not been made subject to any tax payback whatever commensurate to their gains. Some 77% of the budget deficit is being recouped by public expenditure cuts and benefit cuts, and only 23% is being repaid by tax increases. “The plethora of online Photoshop tutorials demonstrate its power and its flexibility, but I believe they also demonstrate its poor design. Think about it like this: what if each time you plunked down in front of World of Warcraft, you had to spend an hour trying to remember, wait, how do I play this?”—

Rands In Repose: Two Universes (via nikf)

Valve are brilliant we can learn interesting stuff from studying their games, but the extent to which this is directly applicable to Photoshop’s UI design is, I contend, negligable.

The main thrust of the argument in the excerpted article is “why can’t Photoshop be more like Portal”. The author actually acknowledges that this is kind of comparing apples and oranges but still many ‘real software engineers’ tend to underestimate the challenges of UI design and dev especially for something as complex as Photoshop.

A comparison with Portal is instructive: I’d argue that the most complex part of Portals UI is actually the movement controls and these aren’t taught by Portal because they’re common across first person console games* after you can move around in 3D space the player has to learn only the following: Fire orange portal, fire blue portal, jump, crouch and general purpose action (flick switches, pick stuff up and drop it). 5 things. You learn all these in the first few test chambers. From then on the bulk of Portal is concerned with showing you how to use these verbs in different relatively un-pressured situations, you only really get to deploy them in a 'live’ situation during the games final 30 minutes or so**. I’d guess this is because the fun of the game is in the learning. Here’s the thing though, the bits towards the end of Portal where you’re reacting quickly to a shifting environment and it feels like you’re freestyling you’re still being directed to find the one solution Valve have set up for you, the fact that this feels like a personal discovery is testament to Valve’s expertise in subtle player direction***.

Photoshop by contrast has hundereds of verbs available to the user and many adverbs which modify their behaviour. I could spend an 8 hour day teaching just the various methods of selection and when each is useful, that’s like twice the entire play time of Portal for just a single category of action. The range of situations to which one might apply these selection actions is utterly vast and, unlike the intricately designed world of Portal, totally outside the control of Photoshop’s developers.

I’m not saying that Photoshop is wonderful, it’s struggling under the weight of its features**** and under Adobe’s decisions to create custom UI chrome, I’m just saying that what it can learn from game design is limited. Comparing Portal to a Photoshop tutorial seems more apt: Here’s a step by step guide to achieving a particular result e.g. removing blemishes from skin/using a portal to launch you across a chasm, now apply it to your own problem/ a more complex or obfuscated version of the same situation. But even in this case the tutorial is likely to be a less than perfect fit for your own problem. 

So in summary, the profusion of Photoshop tutorials doesn’t really show that Photoshop’s UI is badly designed ( it is, but I suspect it’s one of those things that got bad mostly through making a series of good small decisions over a long period of time, it didn’t used to be bad and it’s only changed incrementally over the years ) if you spent as much time learning each available photoshop action as you do each of those in Portal you’d probably be at it for around 10 years and you wouldn’t be sitting down saying 'wait how do I use this thing’. How much fun this process is depends largely on your character and interests: I loved learning Photoshop because of the things it let me do and even in its current sorry state I regard it with some affection.

–

* An evolution of those introduced by Rare with Golden Eye and perfected by Bungie with Halo, Golden Eye was in turn informed by the mouse and key combination introduced by Doom and perfected by Quake. (I guess?)

** Portal is structured like a temple in a Zelda game explore a bit to get your bearings, find a new awesome weapon and learn its uses to get to the end of the dungeon, deploy the wepon in all its ways to defeat the end of level boss.

*** The Left 4 Dead commentary is really interesting in this area, that game is an absolute masterclass in lightly directing a player through a corridor in an apparently complex and confusing environment.

**** As I see it the basic problem with Photoshop’s interface is that the software is so well known that moving or changing anything is likely to enrage a significant portion of your user base (Apple saw this recently with their Final Cut revamp) and the fact that the UI has been accumulating stuff for the last god-knows-how-many years and they’ve had to fit these new features within an existing scheme that may not be suited… well it’s not a job I envy. I’ve been using Acorn a lot recently for much of what I used to use photoshop for and though it has it’s problems (it’s a bit slow on large canavses, very basic range of selection tools) its interface shows a clean well considered alternative to Photoshops ossified layers of cruft. It’s like how you would design Photoshop if you were starting from scratch with the current feature set. (pixelmator’s also good but it’s close apeing of the PS interface is a dissapointment (though many PS old hands will obviously like this))
