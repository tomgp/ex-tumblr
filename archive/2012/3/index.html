“Bauhaus drove craft in materiality, and students developed an intimate understanding of what a given material could do. Painters learned how various pigments “wanted” to flow, and built up tacit understanding of how the physical material would best perform. We too focus on developing a core competency in a given material. But as described above, the “materiality of interactions” are typically people, behavior, and attitudes, and so we drive tacic knowledge of these qualities through both rote and interpretative exercises related to our medium. This demands constant interaction with people, through facilitation, conversation, and immersion, constant reflection on psychology and sociology, and a process of reflection-in-action, in order to consider why the medium of behavior responds as it does to stimuli and to shaping.”—

Jon Kolko » Interaction design and design synthesis. (via ninakix)

Yes! particularly: “constant interaction with people, through facilitation, conversation, and immersion, constant reflection on psychology and sociology, and a process of reflection-in-action, in order to consider why the medium of behavior responds as it does to stimuli and to shaping.”

The value of seeing people using stuff and finding out why they’re using it like they do is totally underestimated. Considering this the raw material of interaction design is something I’ve never done but it makes perfect sense.

“

Back in 1998, websites would often force visitors to aimlessly move their mouse around, trying to reveal hidden icons or pieces of text that would explain where to click. Frustrated with these hidden, obscure navigation elements, web designer Vincent Flanders coined the term «Mystery Meat Navigation».

After downloading and playing around with Apple’s new iPhoto for iOS, I felt like I was teleported back to 1998

”—

ignore the code: iPhoto’s Mystery Meat Gestures

My hunch is that in all but a few highly specialised contexts we’ll take a step back from the pure gestural interface except, opting for a few well defined cross application tasks (eg. scroll, the pinch zoom and a few others) and have some kind of contextual menus (radial menus, has your time finally come?) maybe with gestural shortcuts. The iPhoto for iOS model of increasing app specific gestural complexity doesn’t seem like it’ll scale. 

The popular and slick ‘Clear’ todo list app highlights the problems inherent in purely gestural interfaces i.e. its quite easy to get gestures slightly wrong (I tried it out but was constantly deleting stuff I didn’t mean to, adding items in the wrong place etc. etc. maybe I just have fat fingers but I doubt I’m the only one*) and constantly-do-things-you-mean-to vs constant-'are you sure?’-dialogues.

* actually, just thought, maybe the rise of touch interfaces will bring a broader range of accessibility issues to the fore eg. for people with cognitive and  motor difficulties. For ages accessibility (at least on the web) has basically meant “colour safe? screen readable? your in the clear”. This has always been only part of the picture.

words > pictures

What I reckon:

(words AND pictures) > (words OR pictures)

What I like about Tumblr is its visual culture, as someone who’s not particularly great with words but OK with pictures I feel like it’s a space to stick stuff together and get my ideas in order, a scrap book, I love looking over my /archive and seeing unexpected trend, shifting interests - a birds eye view that wouldn’t really work with text. When I view the daily stream of images/text I feel like there’s space for me to respond in quite a vague way, I don’t have to come up with a solid watertight argument or whatever, it feels more like collaboratively building something. 

On the other hand I wouldn’t want it to be just pinterest or ffffound which always seem like bottomless pits of mild amusement and seem to have a kind of normalising aesthetic effect.

so yeah, pictures plus words.

“Somewhere along the lines, I realized I was looking at everything backward, from an implementation point of view, not from the perspective of a finished application, not considering the user first. And I realized that the inherent bitterness and negativity of programming arguments and technical defensiveness on the web were making me bitter and negative. I’ve consciously tried to rewind, to go back to when programming was a tool for implementing my visions, not its own end. I’ve found that Alan Cooper is right, in that a tech-first view promotes scarcity thinking (that is, making perceived memory and performance issues be the primary concerns) and dismissing good ideas because of obscure boundary cases. And now programming seems less frustrating than it once did.”—

By far the biggest literary offender on this subject, I feel, is steampunk.
