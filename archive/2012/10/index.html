Jack's: Let's reconsider our "users"jacks.tumblr.com

jacks:

During a Square Board meeting, our newest Director Howard Schultz, pulled me aside and asked a simple question. “Why do you all call your customers ‘users’?”

I don’t think this is anything new but…

I think the reason we shouldn’t overuse ‘user’ isn’t because it’s derogatory ( I don’t think it necessarily is. And seriously, it’s a bit dramatic to open with that drug user definition ) but because it’s a really helpful term in a specific context i.e. someone operating a tool. There are lots of other nice specific terms too, all of them helpful in thinking about how you put a website together. Most people using websites will be a user in the narrow sense for some of their visit: They’ll be a user of the navigation, then they’ll become a reader or a viewer, an audience member, a customer or a seller (or a learner or a teacher or a writer or a moderator or a musician or etc. etc. etc.).

I think part of the problem with the design of many websites (I’m thinking particularly news websites here, cos that what I tend to think particularly about) is that the members of the sites audience are thought of by the people making the site as a 'users’ no matter what they’re doing, the sites are optimised for people using navigational tools rather than reading. No one visits a news site to use the navigational tools.

Problem is it’s easy to measure someone clicking around a navigation system so it’s easy to create success criteria and judge against them. Data driven design! It’s a bit more difficult to measure whether someone’s reading something or whether they’ve gone to the loo or are chatting to a colleague let alone whether they have gleaned any benefit from any reading they’ve done “before you leave could you just do this quick comprehension test…”
