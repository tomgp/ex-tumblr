Interview: Mike Migurski - Paul Smithpauladamsmith.com

PS: … Is Spherical Mercator an acceptable enough constraint, from your perspective, within which to do the kind of artistic expression Stamen does, and maintain interoperability?

MM: Oh yeah, I’m absolutely fine with it. It’s one of those “assume a spherical chicken” engineering solutions that actually leads to so much follow-on innovation that in retrospect I’m glad no one was letting the cartographers drive at Google in 2005. Projections only matter when you’re looking at large areas, and that’s really not the case when you’re searching for driving directions or checking out the neighborhood where you spent elementary school. It’s interesting to me, though, that the typical fifty states map you see on every link bait infographic out there is based on a conic projection—it’s about what looks right for a genre of mapmaking, and in the case of slippy maps the Spherical Mercator is clearly the obvious choice.

Russell M Davies: History will remember Samuel Pepys' blog (Wired UK)wired.co.uk

Now, in the world of Twitter and Instagram, it looks even more quixotically patient and focused. And that’s why the completion of Pepysdiary.com should be celebrated – it teaches us that the internet has power over other dimensions than the Social Graph and the Real-Time Web, that web success can be built with things other than venture cash, spammy PR and rapid scaling. Pepysdiary.com has a community because people found it, hung around and started contributing. And it isn’t about making money – Phil does it for nothing, pouring in hours of work every week, out of love, dedication and, I suspect, a cussed determination to see something through. Crucially, it’s not that modern web “for nothing”, which means “no monetisation model now, but we’ll build a billion users and work it out later”. This is the non-commercial, hobbyist web that destroyed so many business models back then and which no one talks about any more.

I’ve read  Pepysdiary.com off and on since 2003, for a few weeks or months at a time and I can’t really say enough nice things about it. Obviously the content is great and interesting and it fits the format is perfectly, and the community of helpful knowledgeable commenters is a rare treat, and the care that goes into how the site works, how everything just fits together in a calm, useful, entertaining and unassuming way, all amazing.

It’ll be over soon and it’s going to feel a bit odd that I can’t randomly check in and see what Pepys was doing 343 years ago. On that day I’m totally going to be drinking a pint of wine in celebration of the site’s creator Phil Gyford.

Blue Lines Revisited: Initial thoughts on thisismyjamtomewing.tumblr.com

tomewing:

I quite like the layout, font etc - it’s not quite as chunkily fun to play with as poor old muxtape was but it’s along those lines. I like the singularity of purpose. I don’t like the “popular jams” but I don’t have to look at them.

I quit This Is My Jam after a couple of months or so of diligently selecting a ‘jam’ each Monday. I followed about 15 to 30 or so people I knew from a variety of internet places and real life, adding a few more each week. The fall off of people using the site regularly seemed quite high among this group though which meant having to follow an increasingly random bunch so that my play list didn’t get too short. Essentially it was just more hassle than it was worth. For a site that demands so little from its users is a bit of a problem I think.

Why did I quit rather than just let my account lie fallow? Well you know how it can feel really good to get rid of physical possessions? eg. I felt a massive burden lifted that time I chucked out Amiga format issues 5 - 40 inclusive; only occasionally do I have mild pang of regret that i can’t have a look at some old ads for 512k memory upgrades or read the review for New Zealand Story or whatever. Anyway, turns out that for me  it holds true for digital stuff too. About a year ago I spent a day deleting social media accounts, extra gmail accounts, hotmail etc. and it felt really good not to have those things hanging around anymore. I often wonder about culling my MP3 collection for similar reasons, do I really need to hang onto those Shellac bootleg recordings or the complete collection of songs used on Disney rides which I have NEVER deliberately listened to? Not quite ready to take that step yet though.

Also yeah, I really miss Muxtape, that was great fun. 

Do you know trntbl.me ?  You just stick a tumblr name after the domain and it’ll play all the audio posts for you through an attractive interface. e.g. for this blog or for 'it took seconds’. I find this scratches a similar listening itch that ’…my jam’ did for me. Without the followers, comments etc. which I can get in a dozen other places.

The Reaction To 'Girls Around Me' Was Far More Disturbing Than The 'Creepy' App Itself - Forbesforbes.com
  • All men are creepy stalkers looking for new digital aids to help them catch and rape women.
  • All women are damsels-in-distress who have no idea how much danger they are exposing themselves to with every Foursquare check-in.
  • “You’re too public with your digital data, ladies,” may be the new “your skirt was too short and you had it coming.”

yes but:

I think the article misses the asymmetry of the app i.e. it’s not  called “girls and/or boys around me”. It’s the difference between a window and one of those two way mirrors (two way? one way? you know what I mean). 

Also, the terrible branding of the app sets up certain expectations as to what the app should be used for - it’s clear the developers aren’t selling this as a way to meet up and form impromptu feminist book groups*.

Design (both interaction and visual elements thereof) is not neutral, people get that instinctively, I suspect this is most of the reason for the outcry - a gut feeling of unease. Looking at the decisions the app developers have taken I don’t think that feeling is unfounded.

Whether the app is more or less creepy than the response is a moot point really. But it seems to me that the app is definitely creepy too.

*not saying that men can’t be in feminist book groups (though I’ve not yet received an invitation :( )
