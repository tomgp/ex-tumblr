“You won’t be able to stop yourself, you might as well enjoy it”: eXistenZ and noncognitive labourmarkfisherreblog.tumblr.com

markfisherreblog:

To appreciate eXistenZ’s contemporary resonance it is necessary to connect the manifest theme of artificial and controlled consciousness with the latent theme of work. For what do the scenes in which characters are locked in fugues or involuntary behaviour loops resemble if not the call-center world of twenty-first century labour in which quasi-automatism is required of workers, as if the undeclared requirement for employment were to surrender subjectivity and become nothing more than a bio-linguistic appendage tasked with repeating set phrases that make a mockery of anything resembling conversation? The difference between “interacting” with a ROM-construct and being a ROM-construct neatly maps onto the difference between telephoning a call center and working in one.

Blue Lines Revisited: Makers and Userstomewing.tumblr.com

(pls click to read the whole thing, it’s short and sweet)

tomewing:

What happens to music before the release date: Making.

vs 

What happens to music after the release date: Using.

…

These are not wholly separable things, they always bleed back into each other. They are not opposed. Mutually dependent, if anything.

But it feels to me that editors, publications, academics, critics - most (not all! maybe not the greatest ones) will have a lean one way or the other, a preference in terms of what they think is interesting, or “important”. They might be alive to some types of argument and tin-eared or dismissive of others. The preference might accent the work they do - is a review, for instance, more of a post-mortem on perceived effort, or a users’ guide to an experience? Is a publication more likely to cut a kvetch about the makers’ authenticity or a sneering aside about the fans?

A really interesting way to think about things; now someone’s said it it seems like an obvious way to look at music writing (and others) - simpler to describe, more easily applicable & less divisive than rockist vs poptimist thing, though as Tom notes definitely related. It seems more general but no less useful/ interesting.

I probably prefer my music writing to focus on the latter but my own reaction to music - i think - is probably more informed by the former.

On the other hand I genrally prefer my wring about video games to be of the how i made this game it school rather than the WoW travelogue school (naratology vs ludology, the r_ v p_ of video game crit?). Perhaps it’s cos I’m mainly a ‘user’ of music but as a programmer of computers (though not generally of games) at least partly on the 'makers’ side in terms of videogames.

cf. 'real literature’ vs 'genre fiction’

Favoured media products 2012

aka what I consumed aged 34-35:

Music. Well I deleted my last.fm account (just spring cleaning, nothing personal) so have no evidence of what I listened to but I feel like it was mostly Grimes (the only new record I bought?), Prince and early Aphex Twin with a liberal sprinkling of Steve Earl. The Minutemen (my favourite band TM) I’ve not listend to a lot. Oh! Also, this house compilation on Spotify and this Paths to Graceland mix. Not much new stuff really, I’m past worrying about that, settling nicely into having embarrassing dad music taste.

Films. Feels like it’s been a pretty terrible year for films (Prometheus and Dark Knight being particular low points) but I enjoyed Cabin in The Woods and Premium Rush. Also, I watched both Aliens and Love and Death again for the first time in ages and both were utterly brill.

Tele. Game of Thrones is seriously, surprisingly good. OK, there are some duff bits, like the whole mysteries of the orient bit, but the central politicking is just a joy. The scenes with Arya Starke and Tywin Lanister are particular highlights but also anything with Sansa, who is brilliantly written and amazingly acted and is perhaps, secretly, the best thing about the show.

Also Homeland has been an entertaining stop-gap playing nicely on audience prejudice before undermining it in a smart way. That said it generally works better on the tense micro scale than on the whole series scale.

Books. Well I was always going to love Building Stories, and The Iliad is fascinating and very readable in Fagles’ translation but my favourite thing I’ve read this year is the new NYRB collection of Robert Sheckley’s short stories Store of Worlds – just utterly overflowing with ideas and humour and fun and humanity,it really is the best. I’ve kind of got bogged down after the third of the four Gene Wolfe Long Sun books, I mean I think they’re good but there’s an awful lot of to-ing and fro-ing in dark and nondescript tunnels.

Also, late entry, I’m finally reading Alan Moore’s Marvelman / Miracle Man courtesy of the Comic Zeal app and bit torrent. I’m only about ½ way through but I pretty much agree with Grant Morrison’s assessment in Super Gods i.e. in terms of super hero psycho-analysis its messy sprawl is possibly the equal of - and in many ways more enjoyable than - the closed mechanical perfection of Watchmen. It’s also kind of ironic that it’s the only one of Moore’s works from his mainstream heyday not to have been plundered by Hollywood and/ or rebooted, back storied, whatever. Y'know, given Moore’s well documented antipathy to all that stuff. It’s been saved by an intelectual property quagmire, the price being that no one (aside from moderately determined nerds) ever gets to read the thing which is a shame.

Oh yeah really didn’t Enjoy Kim Stanley Robinson’s mars books.

Games. Fabulous imaginative golden age platforming with Apple Jack 2 on xbox indie games and Reiner Knizia’s Tigris and Euphrates on our dining table.

“What should I buy?” Well, if you ask me: * Selected Ambient Works 85-92 * Game of Thrones 1&2 DVD * Robert Sheckley - Store of Worlds * Apple Jack 2

PS Stuff I noticed missing: I haven’t seen the Avengers film but my newly unemployed friend Henrietta and I have a vague plan to watch that and all related antecedent Marvel films early in the new year. Dredd obviously, but I’d had enough with comics/ cinema intersection after the Batman debacle. Also awaiting Madmen 5 DVD after xmas.

Happy Birthday Conan The Existentialist, By Stewart Leethequietus.com

Howard’s broadsword-wielding hero Conan would have had little time for the idle musings of post-war French intellectuals, being suspicious generally of the benefits of civilization. But I sometimes wonder if Conan hadn’t already prepared the pre-pubescent me for teenage consumption of Sartre and Camus. In Queen Of The Black Coast, the 1934 novella in which Conan loses his heart to a female pirate called Belit, his doomed lover asks him if he believes in life after death. Conan replies; “Let teachers and priests and philosophers brood over questions of reality and illusion. I know this: if life is an illusion, then I am no less an illusion, and being thus, the illusion is real to me. I live, I burn with life, I love, I slay, and I am content.” Oliver Stone’s movie script made the obvious choice of portraying Conan as a one-dimensional Nietzschean overman. Howard had already shown him to be a two-dimensional left bank existentialist.

“

“The genius of Coursera’s plot to “disrupt” university teaching can, if you like, be imbibed through the medium of a Ted talk. These are lavishly produced speeches, lasting 18 minutes at most, which provide the assembled digerati with a nugget-sized “takeaway”: ideas as fast food. A Ted talk takes the form of a collection of stories tied together by an intriguingly stupid marquee title, forming a tight video-bolus of anti-thought that is then linked around by web enthusiasts.

A perfect Ted talk title is the recent “The Game That Can Give You Ten Extra Years of Life”, by the cybertheorist and “gamification” promoter Jane McGonigal. What could such a game be? Wiring up a joystick to an iron lung? Playing a gladiatorial game of televised chess in which the loser is killed instantly – and winning? No, it’s a little web-based game that McGonigal created called SuperBetter. There are, to date, no large-cohort longitudinal studies showing that SuperBetter makes you live ten years longer, but then a Ted talk is all about attention-grabbing truthiness, not truth.”

”—

Steven Poole’s description of a TED talk in his 300lb smackdown of “cyber-hustlers” like Jeff Jarvis, Clay Shirky, etc. Obviously in these terms there’s not a lot of real difference between a TED idea-bite and a New Statesman essay idea-bite, and I feel like I’ve read this article a few times already. But not with such enjoyable old-school disdain and brutality, so if you like that kind of thing give it a click. (via tomewing)

“a tight video-bolus of anti-thought”

Steven Poole has become a bit of a professional troll but he’s a good one - and I can never have too much of people slagging off Jeff Jarvis.
