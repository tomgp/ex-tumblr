Orange Is The New Black, Episode 10: There’s No Place Like ‘Bora Bora Bora’inthesetimes.com

sadybusiness:

The fantasy that keeps us going.

Recap is up! This time around, we discuss willful self-delusion as a survival tactic, torture porn as consciousness-raising tool, “Scared Straight,” and why you can’t be saved by a lie, not even one that you tell yourself. 

For once we’re actually reasonably up to date with a TV show. We watched this episode last night. This is a great write up esp. this bit =:

It seems to me that the Scared Straight scenes are a pretty good answer to critics who worry that OITNB glosses over the violence of the prison experience. Their inclusion seems to be a deliberate rejection of torture-porn as consciousness-raising narrative device.

Which is spot on.

OITNB can seem quite ‘light’, particularly in comparison to the relentlessly apocalyptic tone of shows like Breaking Bad, for me this just highlights the relentless violence inherent in the prison system. If it was all Oz style grotesque interpersonal violence it would only undermine the idea that people in prison are essentially human beings and the system is about dehumanising them - it would make it easier to say 'oh they’re awful so they deserve it’.

The scenes when Pennsatucky is sent to the psychiatric wing are truly horrifying (I have no idea how realistic these scenes are but …) putting ill people in situations which can only make their illness worse is just fucking barbaric. That’s as truly violent as stabbing someone with a screwdriver or whatever (another trope OITNB neatly disarmed in an earlier episode). Not to mention totally counter productive - see also the solitary confinement bit in an earlier episode which ultimately only serves to make Chapman withdraw any cooperation she might have offered the prison authorities.

So yeah, we’re still a full season behind on Breaking Bad (the best show on tv ™) but I prefer Orange Is The New Black on the strength of the first 10 episodes. Like The Wire before it, it actually addresses the systemic problems, not as an occasional aside (i.e. the healthcare system in BB) , but head on.

Also, it seems of happy to be TV, no cinema envy, the hour is full of dialogue and characters and the episodes have self contained arcs to a much greater degree than a lot of recent 'golden age’ tele. It’s small screen in the best way. If anything it feels similar to ST:Voyager’s later seasons in terms of the ongoing:self contained ratio (though I may just be imagining that cos it’s got Janeaway in it (who’s awesome btw)).

Dramatic End

mammon-machine:

Fans of Fire Emblem swear by it but I’ve found it really, really hard to have much enthusiasm for strategy games with permadeath for the heroes.

…

I get that in theory, a character dying forever could be dramatic, but it’s not inherently dramatic: without resolution, aftermath, or buildup, it’s going to fall flat no matter how well the game has invested you are in the character until that point. Bioware is pretty good about this, but of course character deaths happen at certain predetermined points, so they don’t have to worry about characters dying organically as part of normal gameplay.  

A a way to do this might be to have a character death be a viable option for their “progression” as it were—that there might be an actual reason to do something risky with a character that might have them die forever. Maybe a risk/reward mechanism based on how “dramatic” their death might be like how overwhelming the odds against them are. Or a way to power up in those final moments that would really make their death feel powerful narratively and mechanically. It would be nice for a character death to be a moment you remember personally, one that was unique to the game you alone played, rather than a reason to quit to title and reload.

Xcom kind of manages this well by making the characters randomly generated - so (practically speaking) unique - and just replaceable enough for you not to want to fight though a level again .

The other game that sprung to mind was Vincent Baker’s great tabletop indie-RPG Dogs in The Vineyard (SOOO much I love in the mechanics of that game whilst it still plays as a fully stroy-game style RPG - it’s like a diceless RPG with dice if that makes sense) which provides excellent rules for taking damage physical and psychological in such a way that you don’t feel bad about it happening (and sometimes its really beneficial). I can see this scaling well to a party level RPG: When someone in your party is fatally wounded you can either save them and be in a weakened position at the start of the next battle or let them die and everyone else on the field gets a stat boost or a special ability related to their relationship with the dead character… This would obviously require players to take a greater interest in actually playing the role or creating a dramatic story than might typically be the case in computer RPGs.

The laws that he is talking about are licensing laws and other laws put in place to protect employees, customers, and neighbourhoods. These laws are not all perfect. But the sharing economy has nothing to replace them beyond magical thinking about “trust” (with little accountability).

“The sharing economy is not an alternative to capitalism, it’s the ultimate end point of capitalism in which we are all reduced to temporary labourers and expected to smile about it because we are interested in the experience not the money.
