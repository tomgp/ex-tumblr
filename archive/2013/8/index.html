“Once, when contemplating the apparently endless growth of administrative responsibilities in British academic departments, I came up with one possible vision of hell. Hell is a collection of individuals who are spending the bulk of their time working on a task they don’t like and are not especially good at. Say they were hired because they were excellent cabinet-makers, and then discover they are expected to spend a great deal of their time frying fish. Neither does the task really need to be done – at least, there’s only a very limited number of fish that need to be fried. Yet somehow, they all become so obsessed with resentment at the thought that some of their co-workers might be spending more time making cabinets, and not doing their fair share of the fish-frying responsibilities, that before long there’s endless piles of useless badly cooked fish piling up all over the workshop and it’s all that anyone really does. I think this is actually a pretty accurate description of the moral dynamics of our own economy.”—

On the Phenomenon of Bullshit Jobs | Strike! Magazine (via timoni)

Plenty I agree with in this article. The crux (which is not news but bears repeating):

Say what you like about nurses, garbage collectors, or mechanics, it’s obvious that were they to vanish in a puff of smoke, the results would be immediate and catastrophic. A world without teachers or dock-workers would soon be in trouble, and even one without science fiction writers or ska musicians would clearly be a lesser place. It’s not entirely clear how humanity would suffer were all private equity CEOs, lobbyists, PR researchers, actuaries, telemarketers, bailiffs or legal consultants to similarly vanish.

Blue Lines Revisited: SlideShare and Comicstomewing.tumblr.com

tomewing:

I think it was the planner John Willshere who pointed out a while ago that writing PowerPoint for SlideShare - ie presenterless PowerPoint - was like writing comics. I don’t know if he’s ever written a comic but he had a point, in that SlideShare is a visual/text storytelling medium where (like books/comics but unlike movies/music/presented PowerPoint) the reader has ultimate control over the pace. But SlideShare and comics are coming at pace from opposite directions.

The infinite view thing is interesting, in some ways it seems to be making explicit things that already work/ don’t work in reading comics on a tablet/ iphone etc. A few months Max was going on about how well The Walking Dead which he was gorging on at the time works in that format, largely reckoned because almost everything happens at a per-panel level, so when it doesn’t this offers a simple way to quickly and dramatically change the pace. At the time I was reading a .cbr of Alan Moore’s [Marvel| Miracle]man which goes from working reasonably well in the Warrior issues to not at all in the later Eclipse ones which rely almost entirely on expansive Olaf Stapledon style story telling, the pace increasing imperceptibly but relentlessly over epic double page spreads.

Tangent: Has anyone read that iPad comic Chris Ware did? Reading some of his poster format works (eg. the cover of McSweeney’s 13 and the hardback version of Jimmy Corrigan) can feel a bit like exploring google maps, zooming in at random for detail, zooming out for the overview. That he manages to tie all this non linear reading together - making it meaningful and even emotionally affecting - is a feat which pretty much only he seems to be able to pull off right now. (Though David Aja’s work is seems to be heading in this direction)

“

But billionaires don’t buy newspapers to run them as public goods, and smart billionaires don’t buy newspapers to make money. Billionaires buy newspapers for influence. That is the point. The Post is among the most influential in the nation. Second- or third-most, depending on which party is in power. Buying the Washington Post is sort of like retaining the best-connected lobbyist in Washington, in a parallel world in which lobbyists are universally praised for their value to functioning democracies. Bezos needn’t even exercise his influence in the vulgar fashion of a Murdoch. He can merely staff the paper with people attuned to his worldview and allow the opinion page to evolve to reflect his interests naturally. (And honestly, the interests of a rich “socially liberal” libertarian-leaning Democrat are already pretty well-reflected by the Washington Post editorial page.)

The very reasonable fear isn’t that Bezos will fire all the reporters — he’s a “long-term value” guy, not a cost-slashing Sam Zell — but that Bezos will use his newly bought influence to lobby for federal policies that align with his financial interests. He does not particularly want to pay taxes, and he’d strongly prefer it if his cheap labor remained cheap, which is to say non-union.

When newspapers are only owned by the rich they rarely argue for the interests of the poor. But we already reached that point years ago, and a Bezos is probably no worse than a Graham or Weymouth, in the scheme of things.
