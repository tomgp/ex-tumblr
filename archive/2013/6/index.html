An Introduction to Designer Bullshitvolumeproject.org

  1. No Proof Bullshit. One kind of bullshit is No Proof Bullshit. Many designers commit to claims that cannot be substantiated by easily accessible supporting information, or by a reliable third party. In other words, there is no apparent proof. An example of No Proof Bullshit is Heatwave Radiator by Dutch designer Joris Laarman.

  2. Too Vague Bullshit. Many designers make claims so poorly defined or broad that its real meaning is likely to be misunderstood by the public, press and other designers. An example of Too Vague Bullshit is Bruce Mau Design’s The Bureau of Doing Something About It.

  3. Hidden Tradeoff Bullshit. A third kind of bullshit is Hidden Tradeoff Bullshit, like when a phrase spotlights one aspect of a concept but throws another (usually less complimentary) into the dark. An example is so-called ‘invisibile design’.

Slate | Farhad Manjoo: "How people read online: Why you won’t finish this article"slate.com

katherinestasaph:

rachael-maddux:

Both at Slate and across the Web, articles that get a lot of tweets don’t necessarily get read very deeply. Articles that get read deeply aren’t necessarily generating a lot of tweets.

Read this. (All of it. I suppose I should reference the kicker to prove it.) Reactions: part “ugh,” part “we basically knew this already, insert whichever eye-tracking study making whichever rounds, or maybe that urban legend where the guy hides $1000 in his dissertation” and part “talking about online writing in terms of  pixels is like talking about books in terms of ink expenditure.”

A few quick thoughts re this article:

  1. Compared to what? We don’t really have any data for how people read traditional print media - eg newspapers - in their homes but I’ve always skimmed - first and last pars as recommended by Chomsky in that one bit of Manufacturing Consent - before reading most news articles in full. Anecdata: I’ll read stuff in the review section pretty sketchily even though it’s the only bit of the Saturday Guardian I read from “cover to cover”.

  2. In the article not enough attention is paid to design of the page as a contributing factor to how one reads content - other than saying you have to scroll Salon pages to gett he 1st par. The whole design of the page is set up to a) get people to other pages (right hand column of exciting stuff!) and b) get people to share the page (fixed sharing widget on the left hand side). These are the most important metrics for the publisher to measure wrt to ad sales so designing to maximise people going to other pages and sharing pages is just what’s going to happen. This is I guess a product of the fact that ad sales models based on these metrics are easily scalable (i.e. automatable).

  3. The article itself kind of illustrates why people wouldn’t bother to read the whole thing (in this case i obviously did because a headline like that is just designed to bring out my contrariness). The subtitle is “Why people online don’t read to the end.” but that potentially interesting (if true) point isn’t addressed for the whole length fo the article. There’s no why anywhere, it’s just all “yeah people don’t read to the end” and then a bunch of reckons about “the age of skimming” c.f. point 1 above and also the potted future history bit at the start of The Glass Bead Game (1943) where Herman Hesse goes on about ’The Age Of Feuilleton’.

  4. The most interesting point in the article, the one that that katherinestasaph quotes about not getting to the end before tweeting is not substantiated at all in the data presented because “Chartbeat can’t directly track when individual readers tweet out” so any correlation or lack thereof between number of tweeters and number of finishers can’t really tell us much of anything beyond the very loosest of suggestions.

  5. I have more to say about the pressures that a) cause pages to be designed in an anti-reader way and b) cause the actual content of pages to be devalued wrt presentation but I need to stop typing and go to yoga.
