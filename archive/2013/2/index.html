tomewing:

My main problem these days isn’t strictly procrastination it’s inflexibility. Like, right now I am waiting for a call from a colleague in China. There are a ton of tiny little things I could probably be doing with these 5-10 minutes but it would take me out of my “waiting for a call” headspace and so Tumblr it is.

Yeah. I have quite an abstracted form of this kind of inflexibility procrastination. I find it difficult to start anything new unless it’s on exact hour/ half hour mark. It’s 9 minutes to 11 (just say) so I’ll go and look at twitter or tumblr or whatever, then I’ll start something new at 11. But of course then I’m distracted until 7 minutes past 11 so I think oh well I’ll have a cup of tea and then it’ll be half past and I can start fresh but then it’s half past and it would be stupid to start something new so close to lunch time… etc. I finally get into a rhythm about 40 minutes or so before I need to go and collect children from nursery. At least I leave the work whilst in full flow which is according to Hemingway is a good thing to do.

I learned never to empty the well of my writing, but always to stop when there was still something there in the deep part of the well, and let it refill at night from the springs that fed it.

(As quoted in Reporting (1964) by Lillian Ross)

What Your Culture Really Says - Pretty Little State Machineblog.prettylittlestatemachine.com

We make sure to hire people who are a cultural fit What your culture might actually be saying is… We have implemented a loosely coordinated social policy to ensure homogeneity in our workforce. … The desire to continue being a “culture fit” means it is harder for employees to raise meaningful critique and criticism of the culture itself.

YES! I’ve been interviewed by/ visited a number of organisations over the last six months or so and those who emphasised the importance of a ‘cultural fit’ have been without exception more homogenous (read: white, cis, male, 25-35 ( i.e. “people like me” )) and as a result - from admittedly brief observation - less interesting places to work. Aside from the problems highlighted in the article there’s a particular narrowness of perspective that arises from workplaces with this kind of manicured culture that seems to me a problem in (amongst other things) simple business terms; people making things for other people to use should be striving to broaden their perspective not huddling in a comfortable cultural bubbles.

Who writes well about videogames?koganbot.livejournal.com

koganbot:

The guy in the next apartment wants to know: Who writes well about videogames? Or even not so well?

Define “about” however you wish. Reviews. Anthropology. Etc.

Off the top of my head, stuff I find/ have found interesting:

Versus Clu Clu Land was good whilst it lasted.

Clint Hocking is illuminating about specific mechanics; he wrote an amazing piece about weapon balance in FPS games which I can’t find anywhere (He’s the Far Cry 2 guy so he knows a lot about use of weapons in FPS games)

Tom Bissel’s stuff at Grantland (and elsewhere) is always enjoyable, he’s particularly good on how stories in games work/ fail to work.

Same goes for Emily Short’s Homer in Silicon column which was also good because its subject is often games I’ve never heard of.

Tom Francis’s long game travelogue style pieces are good.

Reviews wise I like Ellie Gibson and Simon Parkin even though the latter generally only review games which I have no interest in playing (JRPGs)

Also Leigh Alexander

Stephen Poole’s _Trigger Happy_ and the associated Edge column are worth a read though a little out of date.

Brenda Braithwaite’s book _Challenges for Game Designers_ is interesting even if you don’t do the exercises.

A banker's millions won't save a life | Business | The Guardianguardian.co.uk

“I am an ambulance medic and part of a two-man crew. We often get up at 4am, check over a 999 frontline ambulance, then drive anything up to two hours to our area of cover, all in our own time. Then follows a 12-hour intensive shift, where we deal with anything from multiple car pile-ups to seriously ill children. We are sometimes abused both verbally and physically. Sometimes we are threatened with sharp implements and have to negotiate or even physically fight for our safety.

Half-hour rest breaks are not always taken due to the demand on services. We then have the drive back, where we have to clean our vehicles before driving home. The stress is monstrous, but we are exempt from any legislation that makes sure we are not worked to death as we are an emergency service. Our average wage is about £15 an hour. We are life savers, counsellors and sometimes just company, as we hold the hand of someone beyond help. We have cultivated a black sense of humour so we do not crumble and, even so, often cry on the way home. I am sure there are many other professions which have their own untold stories of daily physical and mental hardship.

I would like to say to Sir Philip Hampton of RBS that our wages are "modest” for what we do (RBS chief underpaid, says chairman, 12 February). A multimillion-pound pay packet for a banker’s success or failure is not “modest”. We take home in a gruelling year of real blood, sweat and tears what Stephen Hester earns in six days. I wish that those who earn such sums would realise that their renumeration is not right. Perhaps they should not apply terms to themselves like “I have one of the hardest jobs in the world” (Fred the Shred) until they see what others do on a fraction of their wage. What comes out of their mouths undermines millions of hard working people in this country. If an ambulance turned up to one of their children severely injured on a country road, would we seem only worth £15 an hour? As they watched as we fought for their child’s life, far from back up and hospital facilities, would they reconsider the value of jobs that do not make a profit?

Would they consider our wages modest as they apply this term to their own? Modest is a powerful word and has to be earned.

“After thinking about this stuff for a very long time, I’ve settled pretty firmly in the camp of thinking that interfaces should mimic social creatures, that they should have personalities, and that I should be communicating with the interface rather than the interface being an extension of myself. Tools have almost always been physical objects that are manipulated tactually. Interfaces are much more abstract, and much more intelligent; they far more closely resemble social interactions than physical tools.”—

Yours vs. Mine I think I agree. EXCEPT: I would suggest that referring to “my documents” or “your documents” is also a choice, or a fashion, in itself. The idea in both cases is to build a user’s identification with a piece of software either as an extension of themselves or as a helpful friend. Manufacturers of hammers never bothered with this kind of thing.

I recon that simpler labelling like ‘Documents’ or 'Tom’s Documents’ should allow the user to map the interface to whichever bit of thinking stuff (what the article refers to as neurological adaptation which I think is the wrong term for what they’re talking actually about*) they want and also be less obtrusive/ potentially confusing both short and long term. (see Perforce merge conflict resolution where you have to choose between 'theirs’ and 'yours’ sometimes 'theirs’ being stuff you previously committed to the repository and 'yours’ being code written by someone who you’re trying to help navigate the whole car crash).

*I think they’re talking about evolved stuff - my understanding is that neurological adaptation normally refers to changes to a specific brain within the user of said brain’s lifetime.

Why Social Movements Should Ignore Social Medianewrepublic.com

barthel:

I very much enjoyed this piece by Evgeny Morozov, opposing “Internet centrism” and defending centralization, as well as the exchange between Morozov and the target of his critique, Steven Berlin Johnson. The internet’s social prominence has sometimes allowed the folks Morozov is talking about to overlook conflicting evidence within intellectual history. As long as they frame their discussion in terms of commonsense solutions to commonsense problems, they don’t need to confront the instances in which analogous but non-digital examples have been successfully critiqued. The internet - “new media” - makes everything seem strange and different, and utterly disconnected to everything that came before. But media is media, and politics is politics (and communication is communication), and what we’ve found before is likely to still be true. The rhetoric of revolutionary change papers over a lot.

Interesting article.
