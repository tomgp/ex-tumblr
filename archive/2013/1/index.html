Over the Distance矢井田瞳

[This turned out longer than I’d planned.]

It’s an occasional source of mild annoyance for me that there are apparently no good music games available for iOS. Super Hexagon aside there doesn’t even seem to be any games with music that I really enjoy.

It seems like the combination of multi touch and having headphones nearly always plugged is begging for some enterprising developer to pull out the minigames from something like Rhythm Paradise and sell them at £0.69 a pop.

I’d pay significantly more than that for a version of Osu! Tatakae! Ouendan! which seems perfect for a small touch screen device given that it was originally designed for just such a thing. The above-linked song is the soundtrack to one of (IMO) gamings all time high points.

If you’re unfamiliar with the game the best way to understand the action is to watch this youtube clip. So yeah, you control a kind of metaphysical cheerleading squad (dressed in gestapo style leather trench coats obv) and you have to help people overcome the struggles of day to day life by tapping the screen in time with a selection of Japanese pop songs from the last 30 years or so.

The game is surprisingly affecting on an emotional level. There’s something about the heightened mental state induced by walking the cognitive panic/ flow knife edge when combined with simple stories, endearing characters, corny sentiment and un-alloyed pop (esp. pop that you don’t understand the words to - more on that later) which really gets me. I mean I’m kind of sentimental anyway, but the Over the Distance level which has you helping a dead boyfriend move stuff around in his mourning girlfriends apartment totally chokes me up.

This kind of game where your success level vaguely affects a simple, self contained, short story is often maligned for its lack of ‘true’ interactivity but seems to me one of the ways that stories and games work best together. The fact that in Ouendan each level/ story is limited to the length of a pop song helps greatly; the 15 sub 5 min levels of Ouendan pack more story telling – and more effective story telling – than your typical 120 hr JRPG. It also helps that the compressed emotional arc or a 3 minute pop song perfectly mirrors the kind of comic book story being told.

This is where the western remake Elite Beat Agents falls down for me: When the game was in production I was excited to hear that they were using Avril Lavigne’s Skater Boy for one of the levels, I have a soft spot for that song and it seems like having some skater/ teen romance story would totally work, it wouldn’t follow the story of the song too closely– a bit too mean spirited IMO – but you know, same general area at least; boys, girls, skateboards, rockbands, it’s a story as old as the hills. As it turned out the story which the song was hooked up with involved helping a taxi driver get a woman in labour to hospital on time, which is fine, but the dissonance between the story in the song and the story in the game kind of prevents the thing from reaching the awesome levels of the original. Of course I don’t speak Japanese so it’s likely that the songs in the original aren’t thematically linked to their levels – infact it seems unlikely that anyone has ever written a song specifically about a crime fighting race horse. But still, one of the things that pop songs have pretty much mastered over the years is a kind of vague story telling that maximises the possibility for listener identification, its not impossible that the aforementioned vigilante racehorse level is married to a song which involves generally triumphing over a kind of unspecified adversity– it’s not like such songs don’t exist.

People (by which I mean gamer nerds on the internet) are always going on about how the ability to induce an emotional response in the player is like the hallmark of an artistically mature medium and how the way to do this is through complex and subtle storytelling and believable characters. Well maybe*, but the case of Ouendan suggests to me that if you want emotional response then the place to look is pop music; does any area of cultural endeavour seek –and succeed– so consistently to exploit and twist its audiences emotions in laser targeted 3 minute bursts? The synthesis of heart stirring pop music, the brain-punch immediacy of Ouendan’s comic informed visual storytelling and tight, flow inducing interaction design is a great argument for games, at their best, as a kind of masscult Gesamtkunstwerk

Anyway, what was my point? Maybe 1> if you speak Japanese don’t ruin it for me and 2> if you don’t own Ouendan buy it.

* I don’t believe either of these things but this post is already plenty long enough thankyou.

Hobbit opinions

So after weeks (months?) of deciding that I definitely wasn’t going to go and see it ( because 3 films @ 3 hours each !?) and then changing my mind back and forth about twice a day I went to see the Hobbit (2D, 24fps, 9:30am, Westfield*). I really enjoyed it– much more than I expected to.

  1. Was surprised by how much stuff there was that I don’t remember being in the book (that Moria stuff? Radgast?) I suppose if you need to fill 3 films…

  2. Kind of wished it hadn’t been so concerned with foreshadowing LOTR but I guess that was inevitable

  3. Enjoyed the dwarves prat falling, quaffing etc.

  4. Didn’t really like the running battle with the goblins, just seemed a bit too long

  5. I wanted Tom Bombadil to be in the LOTR films though which apparently no one else did so what do I know.

  6. Rivendell is still wrong looking, too much like the ‘interesting’ thing on flickr, all HDR and super natural colour gradients and I don’t know, the architecture is all just too incredible or something but really a small complaint (though be sure you’ll be hearing about it at great length in the pub)

* Weirdly the only other people in the cinema 5 miles from our house were some of our neighbours
